Scoring Methodology

How the Dogpatch Boulders leaderboard score is computed, and the reasoning behind each design decision.

0 Overview

The ranking system uses an iterative, mutually-referential model inspired by algorithms like PageRank. Climbers and climbs influence each other's ratings over multiple passes, producing scores that reflect both difficulty and the strength of the field — not just raw grade numbers.

All data is scoped to a rolling 30-day window, keeping the leaderboard fresh and rewarding consistent activity over historical accumulation.

1 Constants

The system is governed by a small set of tunable parameters. These were chosen to balance grade difficulty, volume, and rarity.

Parameter Value Purpose
BASE_POINTS_V0 1000 Baseline score for a V0 send
POINTS_PER_GRADE 100 Additive points per V-grade level
ELASTICITY 0.5 How much sender strength shifts a climb's rating
SCARCITY_WEIGHT 50 Maximum bonus for rarely-sent climbs
VOLUME_FACTOR 0.1 Geometric decay per ranked send
ITERATIONS 5 Convergence passes for the solver
DAYS_WINDOW 30 Rolling time window in days

2 Base Grade Points

Every climb starts with a base score derived from its posted V-grade. This provides the initial anchor before the iterative solver adjusts ratings.

base_points(grade) = 1000 + (grade_number × 100)

For example, a V6 climb starts at 1,600 points and a V10 at 2,000.

Why linear? A linear scale keeps the model interpretable. A 100-point gap between grades is large enough to matter but small enough that the iterative adjustments (step 4) can meaningfully differentiate climbs within the same grade.

3 Scarcity Bonus

Climbs with fewer ascents receive a bonus, rewarding climbers who send problems that few others have completed. The bonus decays logarithmically — the first few ascents reduce the bonus steeply, then it tapers off.

scarcity(n) = 50 × (1 / ln(n + 1.1))
where n = number of senders
  • A climb with 1 sender yields ~33 bonus points
  • A climb with 5 senders yields ~14 bonus points
  • A climb with 50 senders yields ~3 bonus points
Why logarithmic decay? The logarithm naturally compresses large ranges. A climb sent by 1 person vs. 2 should feel very different, but 50 vs. 51 should not. The log function captures this diminishing-returns intuition without needing arbitrary cutoffs.

4 Iterative Solver

This is the core of the system. Climb ratings and user ratings are mutually dependent — a climb is harder if strong climbers sent it, and a climber is stronger if they sent hard climbs. The solver alternates between updating these two quantities for 5 iterations, allowing them to converge.

Step A — Update Climb Ratings

Each climb's rating is recalculated using the average strength of its senders, blended with the original base grade via the elasticity parameter.

climb_rating = base_points + ε × (avg_sender_rating − base_points) + scarcity
where ε = 0.5 (elasticity)

If strong climbers (high rating) send a climb that was graded low, the climb's effective rating rises. Conversely, if only weaker climbers send a "hard" grade, the climb's rating is pulled down. The elasticity of 0.5 means the adjustment is a 50/50 blend between the posted grade and the empirical signal.

Step B — Update User Ratings

A climber's score is computed as a weighted sum of their top sends, sorted by climb rating descending. Each subsequent send is discounted geometrically.

user_score = Σ (climb_ratingi × 0.1i)   for i = 0, 1, 2, … , 10

In practice, this means:

score = 1.0 × top_send + 0.1 × 2nd_send + 0.01 × 3rd_send + 0.001 × 4th_send + …
Why such aggressive decay? The 0.1 factor ensures that your score is dominated by your hardest send. A climber who sends one V8 should rank higher than someone who sends twenty V4s. The small residual from additional sends still rewards volume, but never enough to overtake a meaningfully harder single send. This mirrors how the climbing community intuitively evaluates ability — by peak performance, with volume as a tiebreaker.

5 Convergence

Steps A and B repeat for 5 iterations. In practice, scores stabilize within 3–4 passes. The mutual adjustment means that:

  • A sandbagged grade (labeled easy but actually hard) gets corrected upward because strong climbers send it
  • A soft grade (labeled hard but widely sent) gets corrected downward because many lower-rated climbers complete it
  • Users who share ascents with other strong climbers benefit from the network effect

6 Movement Tracking

Each time the leaderboard is regenerated, the system compares each climber's current rank against their previous rank. The delta is displayed as a trend indicator:

  • ▲ — Moved up in rank since last update
  • ▼ — Moved down in rank since last update
  • - — No change
  • NEW — First appearance on the board

Design Principles

Three principles guided the scoring design:

  • Grade integrity — Your hardest send should matter most. Volume is a tiebreaker, not a replacement for difficulty.
  • Empirical correction — Posted grades are imperfect. The iterative solver uses the community's sending patterns to surface the actual difficulty of each climb.
  • Recency — A 30-day rolling window ensures the leaderboard reflects current form, not historical achievement. Everyone starts fresh each month.
